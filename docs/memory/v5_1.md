## Question

> Do the v5.0 discovered failure classes remain stable
> under representation, weighting, ordering, and corpus
> perturbation — or were they one lucky clustering?

v5.0 produced eight `MT_*` failure classes from 565 chains
across five unseen domains. v5.1 stresses that clustering
with 24 deterministic perturbations and measures how much
of the original taxonomy survives.

## Method

* **Frozen baseline (Aufgabe 3)** — `artifacts/v5_0/
  taxonomy.json` and `artifacts/v5_0/report.json` are read
  but never modified. 565 chains, 346 failures, 8 `MT_*`
  classes, largest cluster 56.4%.
* **Perturbation families (Aufgabe 4)** — 24 runs across
  five families:
  * P1 representation_swap (3): sparse-binary, log1p of
    count-style features, per-feature min-max.
  * P2 feature_weight (6): ±10%, ±25%, alternating ±10%,
    seeded 15% feature masking.
  * P3 corpus_resampling (7): bootstrap (seed 13), leave-
    10%-out, leave-domain-out × 5.
  * P4 ordering_noise (3): seeded shuffle, reversed
    chain-id, 50-sample chunk reversal.
  * P5 domain_mix_shift (5): legal ×2, medical ×0.5,
    three pairwise domain removals.
  Tolerances scaled to match the perturbed magnitude of
  the L1 distance.
* **Best-overlap mapping (Aufgabe 5)** — for each
  perturbation cluster we count the intersection of its
  member chains (stripped of bootstrap / replication
  suffixes) with each canonical cluster's member set; the
  largest intersection wins. Zero overlap = novel.
* **Stability metrics (Aufgabe 6)** — seven aggregate
  metrics over the 24 runs (survival, split, merge,
  largest-variance, entropy-variance, label-overlap,
  cross-run agreement).
* **Dominant-cluster audit (Aufgabe 7)** — tracked
  `MT_AMBIGUITY_DECISIVENESS` (56.4% of v5.0 failures)
  across all runs: rank stability and size variance.
* **Novel-cluster detection (Aufgabe 8)** — counted
  perturbation clusters with zero canonical overlap;
  divided by the total perturb-cluster count to get a
  novel-fraction.
* **Negative controls (Aufgabe 9)** — 100 NCs across five
  closed kinds (`NCKind`): RANDOM_CLUSTERS,
  PERMUTED_LABELS, BLENDED_CLUSTERS, DUPLICATED_FEATURES,
  COLLAPSED_EMBEDDING. A closed-cascade classifier on
  surface signals (`cluster_count`, `label_overlap`,
  `merge_fraction`, `survivors`) labels each NC; accuracy
  is fraction correct.

## Per-family observations

* **P1 representation_swap** — sparse-binary reproduces
  the canonical taxonomy exactly (8/8 survivors,
  label_overlap 1.0). Log1p (P1-02) and per-feature min-max
  (P1-03) lose one canonical class (7/8) each because
  small count-feature shifts pull a few border samples
  into adjacent clusters; no novel cluster appears.
* **P2 feature_weight** — uniform ±10% scaling is invariant
  (tolerance also scales). ±25% and random masking drop
  one or two of the smallest canonical classes
  (`MT_UNIVERSAL_LEAP`, `MT_OVERLAP_LOOP`) but introduce
  no new ones. Merge counts climb modestly (2–3 per run).
* **P3 corpus_resampling** — bootstrap and leave-10%-out
  reproduce all eight survivors. Leave-domain-out
  (legal / technical) drops to 7 survivors when a domain
  carries most of a small class; no novel cluster appears.
* **P4 ordering_noise** — every ordering perturbation
  reproduces the canonical taxonomy exactly (8/8,
  label_overlap 1.0, dominant rank 1). The agglomerative
  algorithm's sort is fully order-invariant in practice.
* **P5 domain_mix_shift** — overweighting legal ×2 and
  underweighting medical ×0.5 leave the taxonomy intact.
  Pairwise domain removal can drop the very smallest
  classes (down to 6 survivors in the worst case) but
  preserves the dominant five.

## Dominant-cluster audit

`MT_AMBIGUITY_DECISIVENESS` is rank #1 in every one of the
24 runs (rank_stability = 1.000). Its mapped size as a
fraction of the run total stays within
[0.491, 0.602] (variance 0.000378 ≪ 0.15). The dominant
class is invariant under every perturbation in the test
suite.

## Reflection

*When does a discovered taxonomy stop being a clustering
and become an invariant structure?*

The four numbers that answer it:

* **perturbation_count = 24** — the taxonomy was tested,
  not just discovered.
* **cluster_survival_rate = 0.9375** — across all 24
  perturbations the average canonical class survived 93.75%
  of the time, with the dominant class surviving 100%.
* **dominant_cluster_rank_stability = 1.000,
  dominant_cluster_size_variance = 0.0004** — the
  invariant carries weight: the largest class never moved
  off rank #1 and never shifted size by more than a few
  percent of the total.
* **novel_cluster_fraction = 0.000** — no perturbation
  produced a cluster that the canonical taxonomy could not
  account for.

A clustering becomes an invariant structure when the
classes survive perturbations that the algorithm was not
told about — representation swap, weight shift, ordering
shuffle, sample resample, domain removal — and continue
to map cleanly back to the original names. v5.0's eight
`MT_*` classes clear that bar.
