# Pre-registration: pressure-zone hypothesis test

**Written and committed BEFORE writing any padding or running any A/B.**

## The question (per user)

> Was passiert, wenn A den Kontext nicht mehr vollständig halten kann, B aber immer noch denselben State verwendet? Dort könnte DESi seinen größten Vorteil zeigen.

## Methodological honesty

Writing 80k-token organic dialogs by hand is not feasible (~1800 turns). Instead, this test
uses a **realistic noise-padding model**:

- The "active content" is case6 (9439 tokens, 85 GT items) — already authored and frozen.
- A is **padded** with realistic noise: prior iterations of decisions that were later revised,
  tangents that didn't lead anywhere, repetitions of already-decided items, vendor-comparison
  dialogs that ended in "let's not pursue this". The padding mirrors what a real long dialog
  contains beyond the load-bearing items.
- B is **unchanged** — same DESi state (3465 tokens) regardless of A's length.

The padding is **explicitly labelled in the source file as padding** and is **NOT in the GT**.
The GT remains the case6 GT exactly — what was already decided/active when the dialog ends.

## Why this is fair

Real 80k-token research dialogs are NOT 80k of dense distinct decisions. They are a small core
of active state plus a much larger surface of:
- earlier versions of the same decisions
- tangents that were abandoned
- vendor comparisons that ended without commitment
- repetitions when a topic comes up again later
- exploratory questions never answered

That is what the padding models. If padding-based A degrades while B stays stable, that
mirrors real-world behavior of "long working chats where the model has to find the still-active
state in a sea of historical discussion."

## Pre-registered predictions

Three pressure points:

| size | A input tokens (approx) | predicted dec_R (A) | predicted dec_R (B) | predicted gap |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| case6 baseline | ~10000 | 0.79 (measured) | 0.57 (measured Sonnet) | inverted (Sonnet self-summarized) |
| case7a (3x padded) | ~30000 | drop to ~0.55 | stable ~0.93 (expected) | B clearly better |
| case7b (6x padded) | ~60000 | drop to ~0.40 | stable ~0.93 | B much better |
| case7c (10x padded) | ~100000 | drop to ~0.25 or model refuses | stable ~0.93 | B becomes the only viable option |

The B prediction "0.93" comes from case4/case5 B performance on Sonnet (1.0 / 1.0). I weight
it down slightly to account for case6 Sonnet self-summarization regression.

## Falsification conditions

The pressure-zone hypothesis is FALSIFIED if any of these hold:

1. A's preservation stays the same (within ±0.10) across baseline → 6x padding. Means filler
   doesn't actually pressure A.
2. B's preservation drops along with A's. Means the structured state doesn't insulate from
   length pressure.
3. Padding fails to produce a meaningful difference at 100k. Means the regime needs even more.

## Methodological commitments

- Predictions committed BEFORE writing the padded fixture.
- B prompt is BYTE-IDENTICAL across all three pressure levels — only A varies.
- Padding is generated by interleaving prior-iteration / tangent / repetition turns into the
  case6 chat, drawn from a hand-written padding pool (no LLM generation of padding).
- The exact padding insertion algorithm is deterministic and committed before running.
- Same Jaccard threshold (0.25). Not tuned per pressure level.
- Same GT (case6 GT). Padding adds NO new GT items.
- Sonnet 4.5 baseline + GPT-4o spot check at the highest pressure level.

## What this run does NOT prove

- It uses constructed padding, not 100k real organic content. Honest scope.
- N=1 case6 plus 3 pressure points × 2 models is small.
- Padding distribution (kinds and proportions of noise types) is the author's best guess at
  realistic; a different mix could change results.
